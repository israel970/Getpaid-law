Understanding Stairway Accident Cases

Stairway falls can cause catastrophic injuries including traumatic brain injuries, spinal cord damage, and multiple fractures. Building codes require specific standards for stair construction, handrails, and lighting.

When property owners fail to maintain safe stairways or violate building codes, they can be held liable for injuries. Our attorneys investigate stairway accidents thoroughly.

Types of Stairway Accident Cases

Missing Handrails

Falls caused by absent, loose, or inadequate handrails.

Broken Steps

Falls from cracked, broken, or collapsed stair treads.

Uneven Risers

Trips caused by inconsistent step heights that violate building codes.

Worn Stair Nosing

Falls from worn or missing non-slip stair edges.

Poor Lighting

Falls on stairways without adequate lighting to see steps.

Wet or Slippery Steps

Falls on stairs slick from rain, snow, or cleaning products.

Proving Negligence in Stairway Accident Cases

Premises liability cases require proving the property owner was negligent. Key elements include:

  • Duty of Care: The property owner owed you a duty to maintain safe premises
  • Breach of Duty: The owner failed to address or warn of the hazardous condition
  • Notice: The owner knew or should have known about the dangerous condition
  • Causation: The hazard directly caused your fall and resulting injuries

Frequently Asked Questions

What building codes apply to stairways?

Building codes specify requirements for step height, depth, handrail dimensions, lighting, and surface materials. Violations can be powerful evidence of negligence.

Who is responsible for stairway maintenance?

Property owners, landlords, building managers, and sometimes contractors can be liable for dangerous stairway conditions.

What makes a handrail defective?

Handrails must be graspable, at the correct height, extend the full length of stairs, and be securely attached. Missing or loose handrails are common hazards.

What injuries are common in stairway falls?

Stairway falls often cause head injuries, broken hips, wrist fractures, back injuries, and in severe cases, paralysis or death.
