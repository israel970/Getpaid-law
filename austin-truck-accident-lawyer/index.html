Why Truck Accident Cases Are Different

A collision with a commercial truck isn't like a regular car accident. An 18-wheeler can weigh up to 80,000 pounds fully loaded—compared to 3,000-4,000 pounds for a typical car. That's a 20:1 weight difference, which is why truck accidents so often result in catastrophic injuries or death.

But the size difference is just the beginning. Truck accident cases are fundamentally different in three important ways:

1. Higher Insurance Policies = Higher Settlements

Federal law requires commercial trucks to carry significantly more insurance than passenger vehicles:

  • General freight trucks: Minimum $750,000 liability coverage
  • Hazardous materials carriers: $1 million to $5 million depending on cargo
  • Large trucking companies: Often carry $2-5 million or more

Compare that to Texas's $30,000 minimum for regular drivers. More insurance means more potential compensation for your injuries—but it also means the insurance company will fight harder to avoid paying.

2. Multiple Liable Parties

In a car accident, you typically sue the other driver. In a truck accident, multiple parties may be responsible:

  • The truck driver (negligence, fatigue, impairment)
  • The trucking company (negligent hiring, training, or supervision)
  • The cargo loading company (improper or overloaded cargo)
  • The truck or parts manufacturer (defective brakes, tires, etc.)
  • The maintenance company (negligent repairs)
  • The broker or shipper (pressure to meet unrealistic deadlines)

More liable parties means more insurance policies to pursue—and potentially a much larger recovery.

3. Federal Regulations and Evidence

Commercial trucking is heavily regulated by the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration (FMCSA). Violations of these regulations can be powerful evidence of negligence. But here's the problem: trucking companies know this, and they move fast to protect themselves after an accident.

Within hours of a crash, the trucking company's insurance adjuster and lawyers are on the scene, gathering evidence and building their defense. If you wait too long to hire an attorney, critical evidence may be lost or destroyed—sometimes legally.

The most dangerous vehicles on the road. These big rigs dominate I-35, Highway 130, and Highway 290 carrying freight through Central Texas.

Oversized Loads

Wide loads, heavy equipment, and construction machinery on trailers require special permits and extra caution—rules that are often violated.

Who's Liable in a Truck Accident?

Unlike car accidents, truck crashes often involve multiple responsible parties—each with their own insurance.

The Truck Driver

Drivers can be held liable for speeding, distracted driving, driving under the influence, fatigue from violating hours-of-service rules, aggressive driving, or failing to properly inspect their vehicle.

The Trucking Company

Companies may be liable for negligent hiring (failing to check driving records), inadequate training, pressuring drivers to violate safety rules, poor vehicle maintenance, or failing to comply with federal regulations.

Cargo Loading Companies

Improperly loaded or secured cargo can shift during transport, causing the truck to become unbalanced or cargo to spill onto the roadway. The company that loaded the truck can be held responsible.

Truck & Parts Manufacturers

Defective brakes, tires, steering systems, or other components can cause accidents. The manufacturer of the truck or the defective part may be liable under product liability law.

Maintenance Companies

If a maintenance company performed negligent repairs—or failed to identify a dangerous condition during inspection—they can share liability for the accident.

Brokers & Shippers

The companies that hire trucking services can be liable if they pressure carriers to meet unrealistic deadlines, hire unqualified carriers, or overload shipments beyond legal weight limits.

Federal Trucking Regulations That Save Lives—When They're Followed

The FMCSA sets strict rules for commercial trucking. When these rules are violated, people get hurt. Common violations include:

Hours of Service (HOS) Violations

Federal law limits how long truck drivers can operate:

  • 11-Hour Driving Limit: Drivers may drive a maximum of 11 hours after 10 consecutive hours off duty
  • 14-Hour Limit: Drivers cannot drive beyond the 14th consecutive hour after coming on duty
  • 30-Minute Break: Drivers must take a 30-minute break after 8 cumulative hours of driving
  • 60/70-Hour Limit: Drivers cannot drive after 60/70 hours on duty in 7/8 consecutive days

Driver fatigue is a leading cause of truck accidents. Trucking companies often pressure drivers to falsify their logs to meet delivery deadlines. Electronic Logging Devices (ELDs) are now required, but violations still occur.

Drug and Alcohol Testing

Commercial drivers must undergo pre-employment, random, post-accident, and reasonable-suspicion drug and alcohol testing. Violations are common—and can be powerful evidence of negligence.

Vehicle Maintenance and Inspection

Federal regulations require regular inspections and maintenance of commercial vehicles. Drivers must conduct pre-trip inspections and document any defects. Maintenance records can reveal whether a trucking company cut corners on safety.

Weight Limits

Overweight trucks are harder to stop and more likely to cause catastrophic accidents. Federal law limits trucks to 80,000 pounds gross vehicle weight, with specific limits per axle. Overloaded trucks are a serious and common violation.

Critical Evidence in Truck Accident Cases

Time is critical in truck accident cases. Evidence can disappear quickly—sometimes legally. Here's what a good attorney will fight to preserve:

The "Black Box" (ELD/ECM)

Commercial trucks have electronic logging devices and engine control modules that record speed, braking, acceleration, and hours of operation. This data can prove the driver was speeding, fatigued, or driving erratically—but trucking companies are only required to preserve it for 6 months.

Driver Logs and Records

Hours-of-service logs, trip reports, fuel receipts, and GPS data can show whether the driver violated federal driving limits. The driver's employment file may reveal a history of violations or inadequate training.

Maintenance Records

Inspection reports and maintenance logs can reveal whether the truck was properly maintained. Missing records or a pattern of deferred maintenance can be powerful evidence of negligence.

Drug and Alcohol Test Results

Post-accident drug and alcohol testing is required in many situations. The results—or failure to conduct required testing—can be crucial evidence.

Dash Cam and Surveillance Footage

Many trucks have forward-facing cameras. Traffic cameras, nearby business surveillance, and other drivers' dash cams may have captured the accident.

Cargo Records

Bills of lading, weight tickets, and loading documentation can prove whether cargo was properly secured and within legal weight limits.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why are truck accident cases worth more than car accident cases?
Truck accidents typically result in more severe injuries due to the massive size difference—up to 80,000 lbs vs 3,000-4,000 lbs. Trucking companies also carry much higher insurance policies, often $1 million or more. Multiple parties may be liable, including the driver, trucking company, and cargo loaders, each with their own insurance coverage.
Who can be held liable in a truck accident?
Multiple parties may share liability: the truck driver (for negligence, fatigue, or impairment), the trucking company (for negligent hiring, training, or pressure to violate safety rules), cargo loaders (for improper loading), truck or parts manufacturers (for defects), maintenance companies (for negligent repairs), and brokers/shippers (for unrealistic deadlines or overloading).
What evidence is critical in truck accident cases?
Critical evidence includes the truck's black box (ELD/ECM data), driver logs and hours-of-service records, the driver's employment and training files, maintenance records, drug and alcohol test results, GPS data, dash cam footage, and cargo documentation. Much of this evidence can be legally destroyed after 6 months, so hiring an attorney quickly is essential.
How long do I have to file a truck accident lawsuit in Texas?
You have 2 years from the date of the accident to file a lawsuit. However, trucking companies are only required to preserve certain records for 6 months. An attorney can send a spoliation letter to preserve critical evidence before it's legally destroyed.
What are federal trucking regulations?
The FMCSA sets regulations including hours-of-service limits (11 hours driving after 10 hours off), mandatory rest breaks, drug and alcohol testing requirements, vehicle inspection and maintenance requirements, weight limits (80,000 lbs max), and driver qualification standards. Violations of these regulations are powerful evidence of negligence.
What should I do after a truck accident?
Call 911 immediately. Get medical attention even if you feel okay—adrenaline can mask serious injuries. Document everything: photos of all vehicles, the scene, and your injuries. Get the trucking company name and DOT number from the truck. Don't give recorded statements to any insurance company. Contact a truck accident attorney as soon as possible to preserve evidence.
How much does a truck accident lawyer cost?
Most truck accident attorneys work on contingency, meaning you pay nothing upfront and no fees unless you win. The typical fee is 33-40% of your settlement. Given the complexity of truck accident cases and the resources required to fight major trucking companies and their insurers, this arrangement gives you access to experienced legal representation without financial risk.
