The Permian Basin: America's Most Dangerous Workplace

The Permian Basin has one of the highest workplace fatality rates in the nation. The oil boom has brought jobs—but also death and catastrophic injuries on a massive scale. Workers face dangers every day from explosions, toxic gas exposure, equipment failures, and the relentless pressure to cut corners on safety.

If you've been injured in the Permian Basin oil fields, you need an attorney who understands the unique legal landscape of oilfield injuries—and knows how to hold oil companies and their contractors accountable.

Why Oilfield Injury Cases Are Different

Texas is the only state that doesn't require employers to carry workers' compensation insurance. This creates both challenges and opportunities for injured oilfield workers:

  • Many oilfield employers are non-subscribers — If your employer doesn't have workers' comp, you can sue them directly for negligence and potentially recover far more than workers' comp would pay
  • Multiple liable parties — Even if your direct employer has workers' comp, you may be able to sue the oil company, drilling contractor, equipment manufacturers, or other contractors
  • Higher insurance limits — Oil companies and their contractors carry much larger insurance policies than typical employers
  • Clear safety violations — OSHA violations and industry safety standard breaches are common and provide powerful evidence

Understanding Non-Subscriber Employers

About one-third of Texas employers don't carry workers' compensation insurance. In the oilfield industry, many smaller contractors and service companies are non-subscribers. If you work for a non-subscriber and get hurt:

  • You can file a personal injury lawsuit against your employer
  • Your employer loses several important legal defenses
  • You can recover pain and suffering, not just medical bills and lost wages
  • There are no arbitrary caps on your benefits
  • Settlements are often much larger than workers' comp payments

Third-Party Liability in Oilfield Accidents

Even if your direct employer has workers' comp, you may be able to sue other companies whose negligence contributed to your injury:

  • The oil company — For unsafe working conditions, inadequate safety programs, or pressuring contractors to cut corners
  • Drilling contractors — For negligent operations, inadequate training, or equipment failures
  • Equipment manufacturers — For defective drilling equipment, safety devices, or vehicles
  • Service companies — For negligent fracking, wireline, cementing, or other services
  • Trucking companies — If an oilfield vehicle caused your injury

The Deadly Dangers of Permian Basin Oil Fields

Understanding the specific hazards can help identify who's responsible for your injuries.

H2S (Hydrogen Sulfide) Exposure

This deadly gas is present in many Permian Basin wells. At low concentrations, it smells like rotten eggs. At higher concentrations, it paralyzes your sense of smell—then kills you. H2S exposure injuries are often caused by inadequate monitoring equipment, failed alarms, lack of proper training, or failure to provide breathing equipment. A single exposure can cause permanent neurological damage.

Drilling Rig Accidents

Falls from derricks and catwalks, struck-by incidents from tongs, slips, and pipe, caught-between injuries in rotating equipment, and equipment failures claim lives on drilling rigs every month. The pressure to keep drilling around the clock leads to fatigued workers making deadly mistakes.

Pipeline Explosions

Aging infrastructure, excavation damage, corrosion, and inadequate maintenance cause pipeline ruptures and explosions. These incidents can kill multiple workers and cause devastating burn injuries to survivors. Companies that fail to properly maintain or mark pipelines can be held liable.

Fracking Accidents

Hydraulic fracturing operations involve extreme pressures, toxic chemicals, and heavy equipment. Pump failures, wellhead blowouts, hose ruptures, and chemical exposures cause serious injuries. The fast pace and long hours of fracking operations increase the risk.

Tank Battery Explosions

Oil storage facilities accumulate explosive vapors that can be ignited by static electricity, lightning, or equipment malfunctions. Tank gaugers face particular danger when climbing tanks to measure fluid levels. Falls, explosions, and H2S exposure at tank batteries are tragically common.

Rig Collapses and Blowouts

Uncontrolled releases of oil and gas can destroy drilling rigs, cause fires and explosions, and kill or seriously injure workers. Blowouts are often caused by failure to properly monitor well pressure, defective blowout preventers, or rushing to complete wells.

Vehicle Accidents

Oilfield workers travel to remote locations on poorly maintained lease roads. Collisions with other oilfield trucks, rollovers, and accidents caused by fatigued drivers are common. Workers injured while traveling as part of their job may have claims beyond workers' comp.

Wind Farm Accidents

West Texas is also home to massive wind farms, where workers face falls from turbine towers, electrocution, equipment failures, and transportation accidents. The heights involved make many injuries fatal or catastrophic.

Workers' Comp vs. Personal Injury Claims

Understanding the difference is critical to maximizing your recovery:

Workers' Compensation

  • Covers medical expenses and a portion of lost wages
  • No-fault system—you don't have to prove employer negligence
  • Benefits are capped and follow a strict formula
  • No compensation for pain and suffering
  • No compensation for loss of enjoyment of life
  • Can't sue your employer (with limited exceptions)

Personal Injury Claims (Non-Subscriber or Third-Party)

  • Full compensation for all medical expenses
  • 100% of lost wages, past and future
  • Pain and suffering damages
  • Mental anguish and emotional distress
  • Loss of earning capacity
  • Disfigurement and physical impairment
  • No arbitrary caps on most damages
  • Punitive damages in cases of gross negligence

Evidence in Oilfield Injury Cases

Time is critical after an oilfield accident. Evidence can disappear quickly—sometimes intentionally. Here's what an experienced attorney will fight to preserve:

  • Incident reports and investigation documents — Companies often conduct their own investigations
  • Safety meeting records and JSA documents — Job Safety Analyses can prove what hazards were known
  • H2S monitoring data — Gas detector logs and alarm records
  • Equipment maintenance records — Proof of negligent maintenance
  • Training records — Evidence of inadequate safety training
  • Drug and alcohol test results — Both for you and any workers who may have contributed
  • Witness statements — From coworkers who saw the accident
  • OSHA citations and inspection reports — Evidence of known safety violations

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I sue my employer for an oilfield injury in Texas?
It depends. If your employer has workers' compensation insurance, you generally cannot sue them directly. However, many oilfield employers are non-subscribers (no workers' comp), meaning you can sue them directly. You can also sue third parties like equipment manufacturers, drilling contractors, or oil companies whose negligence caused your injury.
What is H2S exposure and why is it dangerous?
Hydrogen sulfide (H2S) is a deadly gas naturally occurring in oil and gas production. At low concentrations it smells like rotten eggs, but at higher concentrations it quickly paralyzes your sense of smell. High concentrations can cause immediate death. H2S exposure injuries are often caused by inadequate monitoring, failed safety equipment, or lack of proper training.
What types of oilfield injuries can I get compensation for?
Compensation is available for drilling rig accidents, pipeline explosions, H2S exposure, fracking injuries, equipment failures, falls from heights, struck-by incidents, caught-between injuries, burn injuries, electrocution, vehicle accidents, and toxic chemical exposure. Multiple parties may be liable, potentially meaning larger settlements.
How much are oilfield injury cases worth?
Oilfield injury cases often result in larger settlements than typical workplace injuries due to the severity of injuries, higher insurance limits, multiple liable parties, and the frequent involvement of gross negligence. Cases involving catastrophic injuries, H2S exposure, or explosions can be worth hundreds of thousands to several million dollars.
What if my employer says the accident was my fault?
Employers and their insurance companies almost always try to blame the worker. But Texas law recognizes that even if you were partially at fault, you may still recover damages. If you were 50% or less responsible, you can recover compensation (reduced by your percentage of fault). An experienced attorney can investigate and establish the true facts.
How long do I have to file an oilfield injury claim?
For workers' comp, you must report your injury within 30 days and file a claim within one year. For personal injury lawsuits, you have 2 years from the date of injury. However, evidence disappears quickly in oilfield cases—companies may dispose of equipment, and witnesses leave. Contact an attorney immediately.
