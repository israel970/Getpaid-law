What Insurance Companies Don't Want You to Know

Insurance adjusters are trained to settle claims quickly and cheaply. Here's how they do it:

  • Quick settlement offers — They'll offer money fast, before you know the full extent of your injuries. Accept too early and you can't go back for more.
  • Recorded statements — They'll ask for a recorded statement hoping you'll say something they can use to deny or reduce your claim.
  • Delay tactics — They'll drag out the process hoping you'll get frustrated and accept less.
  • Surveillance — They may watch your social media or even hire investigators to find reasons to deny your claim.
  • Minimize your injuries — They'll question whether your injuries are really as serious as you claim, or argue they weren't caused by the accident.

A car accident lawyer knows these tactics and how to counter them. They ensure you don't settle for less than you deserve.

Where Car Accidents Happen in Houston

Houston's massive highway system creates countless danger zones. The most dangerous roads and intersections include:

  • I-10 (Katy Freeway) — One of the widest highways in the world, yet still one of the most congested. High-speed crashes and multi-vehicle pileups are common, especially during rush hour and bad weather.
  • I-45 (Gulf Freeway/North Freeway) — Connects downtown to Galveston and The Woodlands. Known for aggressive drivers, drunk driving accidents, and flooding-related crashes.
  • I-69/US-59 — Heavy truck traffic from the Port of Houston makes this corridor especially dangerous for passenger vehicles.
  • Loop 610 — The inner loop circles downtown Houston with high-speed traffic and numerous merge points that cause accidents daily.
  • Beltway 8 (Sam Houston Tollway) — High speeds and distracted drivers make this outer loop a hotspot for serious crashes.
  • Highway 290 — Connecting Houston to Cypress and beyond, construction and congestion create dangerous conditions.
  • I-610 and I-10 Interchange — One of the most dangerous interchanges in Texas with complex merging patterns.
  • US-59 and Loop 610 Interchange — Another high-accident interchange known for collisions.

Houston's unique challenges—frequent flooding, refinery traffic, port trucking, and sprawling suburban commutes—make accidents here particularly complex. Our network of attorneys has experience with Houston roads, Houston courts, and Houston insurance practices.

Texas Car Accident Laws You Need to Know

Understanding Texas law is critical to maximizing your car accident settlement. Here's what you need to know:

Statute of Limitations: 2 Years

In Texas, you have 2 years from the date of your accident to file a personal injury lawsuit. This sounds like a long time, but building a strong case takes months. If you miss this deadline, you lose your right to sue forever—no exceptions. Contact an attorney as soon as possible after your accident.

Texas is an At-Fault State

Unlike no-fault states, Texas allows you to pursue compensation directly from the at-fault driver. You have three options:

  • File a claim with the at-fault driver's insurance company
  • File a claim with your own insurance company (who may then pursue the at-fault driver)
  • File a lawsuit against the at-fault driver directly

An experienced attorney can advise which approach will maximize your recovery.

Modified Comparative Fault (51% Rule)

Texas follows a "modified comparative fault" system. This means:

  • You can recover damages as long as you're 50% or less at fault
  • If you're 51% or more at fault, you recover nothing
  • Your compensation is reduced by your percentage of fault

For example, if your damages are $100,000 and you're found 20% at fault, you'd receive $80,000. Insurance companies often try to inflate your fault percentage to reduce what they owe—a lawyer can fight back.

Texas Minimum Insurance Requirements

Texas requires drivers to carry minimum liability coverage of 30/60/25:

  • $30,000 per person for bodily injury
  • $60,000 per accident for bodily injury
  • $25,000 per accident for property damage

These minimums are often inadequate for serious accidents. If the at-fault driver has minimum coverage, your own underinsured motorist (UIM) coverage can fill the gap.

No Cap on Damages

Unlike some states, Texas does not cap damages in most personal injury cases. You can pursue full compensation for all economic and non-economic damages, including pain and suffering. The exception is medical malpractice cases, which have separate caps.

What Compensation Can You Recover?

Texas law allows car accident victims to recover both economic and non-economic damages:

Economic Damages

  • Medical expenses — Past and future medical bills, including emergency care, surgery, hospitalization, physical therapy, medication, and ongoing treatment
  • Lost wages — Income you've lost while recovering from your injuries
  • Loss of earning capacity — If your injuries prevent you from returning to your previous job or reduce your ability to work
  • Property damage — Vehicle repair or replacement, plus any personal property damaged in the crash
  • Out-of-pocket expenses — Transportation to medical appointments, home modifications, and other accident-related costs

Non-Economic Damages

  • Pain and suffering — Physical pain from your injuries
  • Mental anguish — Emotional distress, anxiety, depression, and PTSD
  • Loss of enjoyment — Inability to participate in activities you enjoyed before the accident
  • Disfigurement — Scarring or permanent physical changes
  • Loss of consortium — Impact on your relationship with your spouse

Punitive Damages

In cases involving gross negligence—like drunk driving or intentional misconduct—you may also be entitled to punitive damages. These are designed to punish the wrongdoer and deter similar behavior.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does a Houston car accident lawyer cost?
Most Houston car accident lawyers work on contingency, meaning you pay nothing upfront. The attorney only gets paid if you win your case, typically 33% of your settlement. You won't pay any out-of-pocket costs for legal representation.
How long do I have to file a car accident claim in Texas?
In Texas, you have 2 years from the date of the car accident to file a personal injury lawsuit. However, if your claim involves a government vehicle or entity, you may have as little as 6 months to file a notice of claim. Contact an attorney as soon as possible to protect your rights.
What should I do immediately after a car accident in Houston?
After a Houston car accident: (1) Call 911 and stay at the scene, (2) Seek medical attention even if you feel fine—some injuries don't appear immediately, (3) Take photos of all vehicles, injuries, and the scene, (4) Exchange information with other drivers but don't admit fault, (5) Get contact info from witnesses, (6) Report the accident to your insurance, (7) Contact a car accident lawyer before giving recorded statements to any insurance company.
How much is my Houston car accident case worth?
Car accident case values depend on medical expenses, lost wages, pain and suffering, and the severity of injuries. Minor accidents might settle for $10,000-$25,000, while serious injuries involving surgery or permanent disability can be worth $100,000 to several million dollars. Texas has no cap on personal injury damages in most car accident cases.
What if the other driver doesn't have insurance?
Texas has one of the highest uninsured driver rates in the country—nearly 1 in 5 drivers. If you're hit by an uninsured driver, you can file a claim with your own uninsured motorist (UM) coverage. If the at-fault driver has insurance but not enough to cover your damages, your underinsured motorist (UIM) coverage can help. An attorney can identify all available sources of compensation.
Can I still recover damages if I was partially at fault?
Yes. Texas follows a "modified comparative fault" rule. You can recover damages as long as you're not more than 50% at fault. However, your compensation is reduced by your percentage of fault. For example, if you're 20% at fault and have $100,000 in damages, you'd receive $80,000. Insurance companies often try to exaggerate your fault—a lawyer can fight these tactics.
Should I accept the insurance company's first settlement offer?
Almost never. Insurance companies make quick, low offers hoping you'll settle before you understand the full extent of your injuries. Once you accept a settlement, you can't ask for more—even if your injuries turn out to be worse than expected. Always consult with an attorney before accepting any offer.
How long does a car accident case take to settle?
Most car accident cases settle within 6-12 months, though complex cases can take longer. Factors that affect timeline include: severity of injuries (cases often can't settle until you reach "maximum medical improvement"), disputes over liability, and whether the case goes to trial. A quick settlement isn't always a good settlement—sometimes patience results in significantly more compensation.
