Can You Still Get Compensation If You're Partially at Fault?

Texas Law • By Israel Medina, Esq. • January 2025

After an accident, one of the first things the insurance company will try to do is blame you—even partially. They know that if they can shift some fault onto you, they'll pay out less money.

But here's the good news: in Texas, you can still recover compensation even if you were partially at fault. You just need to understand how the rules work.

Texas Uses "Modified Comparative Fault"

Texas follows what's called a modified comparative fault rule (also known as "proportionate responsibility"). Here's how it works:

  • Your compensation is reduced by your percentage of fault
  • If you're 50% or less at fault, you can still recover damages
  • If you're 51% or more at fault, you get nothing

That 51% threshold is crucial. It's the difference between getting a reduced payout and getting nothing at all.

The 51% Rule: As long as you're not more than 50% responsible for the accident, you can still recover compensation. But if you're found 51% or more at fault, you're barred from recovering anything—even if you have $500,000 in medical bills.

How Comparative Fault Works: Real Examples

Example 1: You're 20% at Fault

You're rear-ended at a stoplight, but your brake lights were out. The jury finds you 20% at fault and the other driver 80% at fault.

Your damages: $100,000

Your recovery: $100,000 - 20% = $80,000

Example 2: You're 50% at Fault

You ran a yellow light and were T-boned by someone who was speeding. The jury splits fault 50/50.

Your damages: $100,000

Your recovery: $100,000 - 50% = $50,000

Example 3: You're 51% at Fault

Same scenario, but the jury finds you 51% at fault.

Your damages: $100,000

Your recovery: $0

See how that works? The difference between 50% and 51% fault is the difference between $50,000 and nothing.

How Insurance Companies Use This Against You

Insurance adjusters are trained to gather evidence that shifts blame onto you. They might:

  • Take recorded statements and ask leading questions designed to get you to admit fault
  • Dig into your driving history looking for past tickets or accidents
  • Claim you were speeding, distracted, or failed to avoid the accident
  • Use your social media posts against you
  • Hire accident reconstruction experts to argue you were more at fault

Their goal is simple: get your fault percentage as high as possible. If they can push you past 50%, they pay nothing. If they can't, they still reduce what they owe.

Warning: Never give a recorded statement to the other driver's insurance company without talking to a lawyer first. What feels like a routine conversation is actually an investigation designed to minimize your claim.

Common Ways They'll Try to Blame You

In Car Accidents

  • You were going a few mph over the speed limit
  • You didn't brake soon enough
  • You were on your phone (even if you weren't)
  • You made a lane change without signaling
  • Your vehicle had a maintenance issue (worn tires, broken lights)

In Slip and Fall Cases

  • You weren't watching where you were going
  • You were wearing inappropriate footwear
  • You ignored warning signs
  • You were on your phone while walking
  • The hazard was "open and obvious"

In Workplace Injuries

  • You weren't following safety procedures
  • You weren't wearing proper protective equipment
  • You were "horsing around"
  • You were working outside your training

How to Protect Yourself

If you've been injured and there's any question about fault, here's what you should do:

  1. Don't admit fault at the scene. Stick to the facts. Don't apologize or speculate about what happened.
  2. Document everything. Photos, witness contact info, police report—all of this helps establish what really happened.
  3. Don't give recorded statements to the other party's insurance without legal advice.
  4. Stay off social media. Insurance companies will comb through your posts looking for anything they can use.
  5. Talk to a lawyer. An experienced personal injury attorney knows how to counter the insurance company's blame-shifting tactics.

Why Having a Lawyer Matters

Fault is rarely black and white. It's determined by evidence, witness testimony, expert analysis, and legal arguments. The insurance company has a team of professionals working to maximize your fault percentage. You need someone in your corner doing the opposite.

A good personal injury lawyer will:

  • Investigate the accident and gather evidence that supports your case
  • Counter the insurance company's attempts to shift blame
  • Hire expert witnesses if needed (accident reconstructionists, medical experts)
  • Negotiate from a position of strength
  • Take the case to trial if the insurance company won't offer fair compensation

The Bottom Line

Being partially at fault doesn't mean you can't recover compensation in Texas. As long as you're 50% or less responsible, you still have a valid claim—it's just reduced by your percentage of fault.

But the insurance company will do everything they can to push that percentage higher. Don't let them define the narrative without fighting back.
