How Long Do You Have to File a Personal Injury Claim in Texas?

Texas Law • By Israel Medina, Esq. • January 2025

In Texas, you have a limited window of time to file a personal injury lawsuit. Miss that deadline—even by one day—and you lose your right to sue, no matter how strong your case is.

This deadline is called the statute of limitations, and understanding it is critical to protecting your right to compensation.

The General Rule: 2 Years

For most personal injury cases in Texas, you have 2 years from the date of the injury to file a lawsuit. This applies to:

  • Car accidents
  • Truck accidents
  • Motorcycle accidents
  • Slip and fall accidents
  • Dog bites
  • Assault and battery
  • Most other negligence claims

Important: The 2-year clock starts ticking on the date of the accident—not when you realize how serious your injuries are. Don't wait until you "feel better" to talk to a lawyer.

Different Deadlines for Different Cases

Not all cases follow the 2-year rule. Here are the key exceptions:

Type of Case Deadline
Personal injury (general) 2 years
Car/truck/motorcycle accidents 2 years
Wrongful death 2 years from date of death
Medical malpractice 2 years (with 10-year maximum)
Claims against government entities 6 months notice required
Product liability 2 years (15-year maximum)

Special Rules That Can Affect Your Deadline

Claims Against the Government

If your injury was caused by a government entity—like a city bus, a state employee, or a poorly maintained public road—the rules are much stricter. Under the Texas Tort Claims Act, you must provide formal written notice within 6 months of the incident.

Miss this notice deadline, and your claim is likely dead before it starts.

The Discovery Rule

In some cases, you might not know you were injured right away. For example, if a doctor left a surgical instrument inside you, you wouldn't know until symptoms appeared or another doctor found it.

Texas courts sometimes apply the "discovery rule," which starts the clock when you knew or should have known about the injury. But this exception is narrow and hard to prove—don't count on it without talking to a lawyer.

Minors and Incapacitated Persons

If the injured person is under 18, the statute of limitations is "tolled" (paused) until they turn 18. So a child injured at age 10 would have until age 20 to file.

Similar rules apply if the person is mentally incapacitated at the time of the injury.

What Happens If You Miss the Deadline?

If you try to file a lawsuit after the statute of limitations has expired, the defendant will file a motion to dismiss, and the court will almost certainly grant it. Your case will be thrown out, regardless of:

  • How badly you were injured
  • How clearly the other party was at fault
  • How much evidence you have

This is why it's so important to act quickly. Even if you're not sure whether you have a case, talking to a lawyer early protects your options.

Why You Shouldn't Wait Until the Last Minute

Even though you technically have 2 years, waiting is a bad idea for several reasons:

  • Evidence disappears. Witnesses forget details, surveillance footage gets deleted, and physical evidence gets lost or repaired.
  • Medical records matter. The longer you wait to document your injuries, the easier it is for insurance companies to claim your injuries weren't caused by the accident.
  • Building a case takes time. Your attorney needs time to investigate, gather evidence, negotiate with insurance companies, and prepare for trial if necessary.
  • Insurance companies know the deadline. If you wait too long, they may stall negotiations knowing you're running out of time to file suit.

Our advice: Talk to a personal injury lawyer as soon as possible after your accident. Most offer free consultations, and you'll know exactly where you stand before any deadlines become an issue.

The Bottom Line

In Texas, you generally have 2 years to file a personal injury lawsuit—but there are important exceptions that could shorten that window. Don't gamble with your right to compensation.
