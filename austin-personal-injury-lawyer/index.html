What Compensation Can You Recover?

Texas law allows personal injury victims to recover damages for:

  • Medical expenses — Emergency room visits, surgery, hospitalization, physical therapy, and future medical care
  • Lost wages — Income you've lost while recovering, plus future earning capacity if you can't return to work
  • Pain and suffering — Physical pain, emotional distress, and reduced quality of life
  • Property damage — Vehicle repairs or replacement
  • Wrongful death damages — If you've lost a loved one, you may be entitled to funeral expenses, loss of companionship, and more

Texas has no cap on personal injury damages in most cases, meaning you can pursue full compensation for everything you've lost.

Understanding Texas Personal Injury Law

Texas has specific laws that affect your personal injury case. Here's what you need to know:

Statute of Limitations

In Texas, you generally have 2 years from the date of your accident to file a personal injury lawsuit. Miss this deadline and you lose your right to compensation forever. Some exceptions exist—claims against government entities have shorter deadlines (often 6 months for notice)—which is why consulting with an attorney quickly is critical.

Modified Comparative Fault

Texas follows a "51% rule." You can recover damages as long as you're not more than 50% at fault for the accident. However, your compensation is reduced by your percentage of fault. For example, if you're awarded $100,000 but found 20% at fault, you'd receive $80,000.

No-Fault Insurance? Not in Texas

Texas is an "at-fault" state, meaning the person who caused the accident is responsible for damages. You can file a claim against the at-fault driver's insurance, file through your own insurance, or sue the at-fault driver directly.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does an Austin personal injury lawyer cost?
Most Austin personal injury lawyers work on contingency, meaning you pay nothing upfront and no fees unless you win your case. The typical contingency fee is 33% of your settlement, though this can vary. You'll never pay out of pocket for legal representation.
How long do I have to file a personal injury claim in Texas?
In Texas, the statute of limitations for most personal injury claims is 2 years from the date of the accident. However, some cases have shorter deadlines—claims against government entities may require notice within 6 months. It's important to consult with an attorney as soon as possible to protect your rights.
What should I do after a car accident in Austin?
After an Austin car accident: (1) Call 911 and report the accident, (2) Seek medical attention even if you feel fine—some injuries don't appear immediately, (3) Document the scene with photos of all vehicles, injuries, and road conditions, (4) Exchange information with other drivers, (5) Do not admit fault or apologize, (6) Contact a personal injury attorney before speaking with insurance companies.
How much is my Austin personal injury case worth?
Case value depends on many factors: severity of injuries, medical expenses, lost wages, impact on quality of life, and the strength of evidence. Minor soft tissue injuries might settle for $10,000-$25,000, while serious injuries involving surgery or permanent disability can be worth hundreds of thousands or more. An experienced Austin personal injury attorney can evaluate your specific situation during a free consultation.
Should I accept the insurance company's settlement offer?
Almost never—at least not without consulting an attorney first. Insurance companies make quick, lowball offers hoping you'll accept before you understand the full extent of your injuries. Once you accept, you can't go back for more. An attorney can evaluate whether the offer is fair and negotiate for the compensation you actually deserve.
What if the other driver doesn't have insurance?
If you were hit by an uninsured or underinsured driver in Austin, you may still have options. Your own uninsured/underinsured motorist coverage can provide compensation. An attorney can help you understand your policy and pursue all available sources of recovery.
