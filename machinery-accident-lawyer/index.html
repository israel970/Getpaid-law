Why You Need a Machinery Accident Lawyer

Industrial machinery can cause devastating injuries in an instant. Conveyor belts, presses, lathes, and other heavy equipment can crush, amputate, or kill workers when proper safety guards are missing or bypassed.

Employers have a duty to provide safe machinery with proper guards, emergency stops, and lockout/tagout procedures. When they fail, victims deserve full compensation for their injuries.

Types of Machinery Accident Cases

Press Injuries

Crushing and amputation injuries from hydraulic presses, punch presses, and mechanical presses.

Conveyor Belt Accidents

Workers caught in conveyor systems, causing crush injuries and amputations.

Lathe Accidents

Entanglement injuries from rotating lathes and CNC machines.

Forklift Accidents

Workers struck by, run over by, or crushed by forklifts and pallet jacks.

Packaging Equipment

Injuries from industrial packaging, boxing, and palletizing machinery.

Agricultural Equipment

Farm machinery accidents involving tractors, combines, and harvesters.

Proving Liability in Machinery Accident Cases

Establishing liability in workplace injury cases requires thorough investigation and understanding of OSHA regulations and industry standards. Key elements we examine include:

  • Employer Negligence: Failure to provide safe working conditions, proper training, or required safety equipment
  • OSHA Violations: Documented safety violations that contributed to your injury
  • Equipment Defects: Faulty machinery or tools that caused or contributed to the accident
  • Third-Party Liability: Contractors, property owners, or manufacturers who share responsibility

Frequently Asked Questions

What safety features should machinery have?

Machine guards, emergency stops, lockout/tagout systems, warning labels, and proper training are required.

Who is liable for a machinery accident?

Employers, equipment manufacturers, maintenance companies, and safety consultants may all be liable.

What is lockout/tagout?

Lockout/tagout procedures ensure machinery is properly shut down and cannot be restarted during maintenance. Violations are common causes of injuries.

Can I sue the equipment manufacturer?

Yes, if the machinery was defectively designed or manufactured, or lacked adequate warnings or safety features.
