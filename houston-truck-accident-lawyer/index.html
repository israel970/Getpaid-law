Why Houston Truck Accident Cases Are Different

Houston is the trucking capital of Texas. The Port of Houston—one of the busiest ports in America—sends thousands of container trucks onto Houston highways every day. Add refinery traffic, oil field equipment, and cross-country freight, and you have a recipe for serious accidents.

A collision with a commercial truck isn't like a regular car accident. An 18-wheeler can weigh up to 80,000 pounds fully loaded—compared to 3,000-4,000 pounds for a typical car. That's a 20:1 weight difference, which is why truck accidents so often result in catastrophic injuries or death.

Port of Houston Trucking

The Port of Houston handles more foreign tonnage than any other U.S. port. This means:

  • Constant container truck traffic on Highway 225, I-610, and I-10
  • Overloaded containers that exceed weight limits and cause accidents
  • Tired drivers racing to meet shipping deadlines
  • Multiple liable parties including shipping companies, port operators, and trucking contractors

Refinery and Chemical Haulers

Houston's Ship Channel is lined with refineries and chemical plants. Tanker trucks carrying hazardous materials create additional dangers:

  • Chemical spills and toxic exposure in accidents
  • Fire and explosion risks
  • Specialized safety regulations that are frequently violated
  • Higher insurance policies due to hazardous cargo

Higher Insurance Policies = Higher Settlements

Federal law requires commercial trucks to carry significantly more insurance than passenger vehicles:

  • General freight trucks: Minimum $750,000 liability coverage
  • Hazardous materials carriers: $1 million to $5 million depending on cargo
  • Large trucking companies: Often carry $2-5 million or more

Compare that to Texas's $30,000 minimum for regular drivers. More insurance means more potential compensation for your injuries—but it also means the insurance company will fight harder to avoid paying.

Types of Commercial Trucks in Houston

Our network handles accidents involving all types of commercial vehicles on Houston roads.

Port Container Trucks

Massive trucks hauling shipping containers from the Port of Houston. Heavy loads, tight schedules, and overloaded containers cause serious accidents.

18-Wheelers / Semi-Trucks

The most dangerous vehicles on the road. These big rigs dominate I-10, I-45, and I-69 carrying freight across Texas and the nation.

Tanker Trucks

Tankers carrying fuel, chemicals, or other hazardous materials from Houston refineries can cause catastrophic accidents including fires and toxic exposure.

Oil Field Equipment

Heavy equipment, pipe, and machinery transported to and from oil fields. Oversized loads and poorly secured cargo create serious hazards.

Delivery Trucks

Amazon, FedEx, UPS, and other delivery vehicles are everywhere—and their drivers face intense pressure to meet delivery quotas.

Concrete Mixers

With Houston's constant construction, cement trucks are everywhere. Their high center of gravity makes them prone to rollovers.

Dump Trucks

Common on construction sites and highways throughout Houston. Unsecured loads can spill debris into traffic.

Garbage & Waste Trucks

These heavy trucks make frequent stops and have massive blind spots. They're involved in more accidents than most people realize.

Oversized Loads

Wide loads, heavy equipment, and construction machinery on trailers require special permits and extra caution—rules that are often violated.

Who's Liable in a Houston Truck Accident?

Unlike car accidents, truck crashes often involve multiple responsible parties—each with their own insurance. This is especially true in Houston's complex shipping and refinery industries.

The Truck Driver

Drivers can be held liable for speeding, distracted driving, driving under the influence, fatigue from violating hours-of-service rules, aggressive driving, or failing to properly inspect their vehicle.

The Trucking Company

Companies may be liable for negligent hiring (failing to check driving records), inadequate training, pressuring drivers to violate safety rules, poor vehicle maintenance, or failing to comply with federal regulations.

Shipping & Logistics Companies

In Houston's port industry, shipping companies, freight brokers, and logistics providers may share liability for accidents caused by unrealistic deadlines or improper cargo handling.

Cargo Loading Companies

Improperly loaded or secured cargo can shift during transport, causing the truck to become unbalanced or cargo to spill onto the roadway. The company that loaded the truck can be held responsible.

Truck & Parts Manufacturers

Defective brakes, tires, steering systems, or other components can cause accidents. The manufacturer of the truck or the defective part may be liable under product liability law.

Refineries & Chemical Companies

When tanker trucks are involved, the refinery or chemical company that loaded hazardous materials may share liability, especially if safety protocols weren't followed.

Federal Trucking Regulations That Save Lives—When They're Followed

The FMCSA sets strict rules for commercial trucking. When these rules are violated, people get hurt. Common violations include:

Hours of Service (HOS) Violations

Federal law limits how long truck drivers can operate:

  • 11-Hour Driving Limit: Drivers may drive a maximum of 11 hours after 10 consecutive hours off duty
  • 14-Hour Limit: Drivers cannot drive beyond the 14th consecutive hour after coming on duty
  • 30-Minute Break: Drivers must take a 30-minute break after 8 cumulative hours of driving
  • 60/70-Hour Limit: Drivers cannot drive after 60/70 hours on duty in 7/8 consecutive days

Driver fatigue is a leading cause of truck accidents. Port trucking companies and refineries often pressure drivers to falsify their logs to meet shipping deadlines. Electronic Logging Devices (ELDs) are now required, but violations still occur.

Hazardous Materials Regulations

Houston's chemical and refinery industry creates additional regulations:

  • Special training and endorsements for hazmat drivers
  • Strict placarding and documentation requirements
  • Route restrictions for hazardous cargo
  • Enhanced insurance requirements

Vehicle Maintenance and Inspection

Federal regulations require regular inspections and maintenance of commercial vehicles. Drivers must conduct pre-trip inspections and document any defects. Maintenance records can reveal whether a trucking company cut corners on safety.

Weight Limits

Overweight trucks are harder to stop and more likely to cause catastrophic accidents. Federal law limits trucks to 80,000 pounds gross vehicle weight, with specific limits per axle. Port containers are frequently overloaded—a serious and common violation.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why are truck accident cases worth more than car accident cases?
Truck accidents typically result in more severe injuries due to the massive size difference—up to 80,000 lbs vs 3,000-4,000 lbs. Trucking companies also carry much higher insurance policies, often $1 million or more. In Houston, port shipping companies and refineries add additional layers of insurance coverage. Multiple parties may be liable, each with their own insurance.
Who can be held liable in a Houston truck accident?
Multiple parties may share liability: the truck driver, the trucking company, shipping/logistics companies, cargo loaders, truck or parts manufacturers, maintenance companies, and in hazmat cases, refineries or chemical companies. More liable parties means more insurance policies to pursue.
What evidence is critical in truck accident cases?
Critical evidence includes the truck's black box (ELD/ECM data), driver logs and hours-of-service records, the driver's employment and training files, maintenance records, drug and alcohol test results, GPS data, dash cam footage, and cargo documentation. Much of this evidence can be legally destroyed after 6 months, so hiring an attorney quickly is essential.
How long do I have to file a truck accident lawsuit in Texas?
You have 2 years from the date of the accident to file a lawsuit. However, trucking companies are only required to preserve certain records for 6 months. An attorney can send a spoliation letter to preserve critical evidence before it's legally destroyed.
What should I do after a truck accident in Houston?
Call 911 immediately. Get medical attention even if you feel okay—adrenaline can mask serious injuries. Document everything: photos of all vehicles, the scene, and your injuries. Get the trucking company name and DOT number from the truck. Don't give recorded statements to any insurance company. Contact a truck accident attorney as soon as possible to preserve evidence.
How much does a truck accident lawyer cost?
Most truck accident attorneys work on contingency, meaning you pay nothing upfront and no fees unless you win. The typical fee is 33-40% of your settlement. Given the complexity of truck accident cases and the resources required to fight major trucking companies and their insurers, this arrangement gives you access to experienced legal representation without financial risk.
