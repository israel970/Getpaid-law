Understanding Wrongful Death Claims in Texas

A wrongful death occurs when someone dies due to another person's or company's negligence, recklessness, or intentional act. Texas law allows surviving family members to seek compensation for their loss—both for the financial impact and the emotional devastation.

These cases are deeply personal, but they're also legally complex. You need an attorney who can provide compassionate support while aggressively pursuing justice for your family.

Who Can File a Wrongful Death Lawsuit in Texas?

Under Texas law, only certain family members can bring a wrongful death claim:

  • Surviving spouse
  • Children (biological or adopted)
  • Parents

These family members can file individually or together. Siblings, grandparents, and other relatives generally cannot file wrongful death claims in Texas, though there may be other legal options available.

If no eligible family member files a lawsuit within three months of the death, the personal representative of the deceased's estate may file on behalf of the family.

Wrongful Death vs. Survival Action

Texas recognizes two related but distinct claims:

  • Wrongful death claims compensate family members for their losses—the financial support, companionship, and guidance they've lost
  • Survival actions compensate the deceased's estate for the pain, suffering, and medical expenses the person experienced before death

Many cases involve both types of claims, which are often combined in a single lawsuit.

Common Causes of Wrongful Death in Houston

Houston's industrial landscape creates unique wrongful death risks not seen in other Texas cities.

Refinery Explosions

Houston's petrochemical corridor sees tragic explosions and fires. Workers and nearby residents can be killed by blasts, fires, and toxic releases.

Industrial Accidents

Chemical plants, manufacturing facilities, and port operations create hazardous conditions that can result in fatal workplace incidents.

Truck Accidents

Port trucking, refinery tankers, and 18-wheelers on Houston's congested highways cause fatal collisions daily.

Car Accidents

Houston's dangerous highway system—I-10, I-45, I-69, Loop 610, Beltway 8—sees fatal crashes from drunk, distracted, and reckless drivers.

Port & Maritime Deaths

The Port of Houston is one of the busiest in the nation. Crane accidents, cargo incidents, and vessel accidents claim lives.

Construction Fatalities

Houston's construction boom means more falls, crane accidents, electrocutions, and struck-by deaths on job sites.

Medical Malpractice

Even at the world-renowned Texas Medical Center, surgical errors, misdiagnosis, and medication mistakes can have fatal consequences.

Chemical Exposure Deaths

Long-term exposure to toxic chemicals at refineries and plants can cause fatal cancers and respiratory diseases.

Flood-Related Deaths

Houston's frequent flooding claims lives in vehicle drownings, building collapses, and electrocutions. Property owners may be liable for unsafe conditions.

Compensation in Texas Wrongful Death Cases

Texas law allows families to recover compensation for both economic and non-economic losses:

Economic Damages

  • Loss of financial support — The income and benefits the deceased would have provided to the family
  • Loss of services — The value of household services, childcare, and other contributions
  • Funeral and burial expenses
  • Medical expenses — Bills incurred from the injury until death
  • Loss of inheritance — The savings and assets the deceased would have accumulated

Non-Economic Damages

  • Loss of companionship — The love, comfort, and society of the deceased
  • Loss of guidance — Particularly important for surviving children
  • Mental anguish — The emotional pain and suffering of family members
  • Loss of consortium — The loss of the marital relationship for surviving spouses

Holding Houston Companies Accountable

When major corporations—refineries, chemical plants, shipping companies, construction firms—cause a death through negligence, they have teams of lawyers ready to minimize their liability. You need attorneys with the resources and experience to take on these powerful entities and hold them accountable.

Statute of Limitations

Texas has a 2-year statute of limitations for wrongful death claims, beginning from the date of death. If you miss this deadline, you lose your right to file a lawsuit forever. Some situations—such as claims against government entities—have shorter deadlines. Contact an attorney as soon as you're able to ensure your rights are protected.

Frequently Asked Questions

Who can file a wrongful death lawsuit in Texas?
Under Texas law, only the surviving spouse, children, and parents of the deceased can file a wrongful death lawsuit. These family members can file individually or together. If no family member files within 3 months, the estate's executor may file on behalf of the estate.
What damages can be recovered in a Texas wrongful death case?
Texas allows recovery of pecuniary damages including loss of financial support, loss of services and guidance, loss of companionship and consortium, mental anguish and emotional pain, and funeral and burial expenses. The estate can also recover medical expenses and lost wages from the time of injury to death.
How long do I have to file a wrongful death lawsuit in Texas?
Texas has a 2-year statute of limitations for wrongful death claims, starting from the date of death. If the claim involves a government entity, special notice requirements may apply with shorter deadlines. It's important to consult an attorney promptly to preserve your rights.
Can I afford a wrongful death attorney?
Wrongful death attorneys work on contingency, meaning you pay nothing upfront and no fees unless you win. This allows grieving families to access experienced legal representation without financial burden during an already difficult time.
Can I sue a refinery or chemical plant for a death?
Yes. When refineries, chemical plants, or other industrial facilities cause deaths through explosions, toxic releases, fires, or other negligence, they can be held liable. These companies have extensive resources to fight claims, so you need experienced attorneys who can match their legal firepower.
