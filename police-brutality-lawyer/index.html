Fighting for Victims of Police Misconduct

Police officers are entrusted with enormous power—the authority to detain, arrest, and in some cases, use deadly force. When that power is abused, the consequences can be devastating: physical injuries, emotional trauma, wrongful imprisonment, and in the worst cases, loss of life.

If you or a loved one has been a victim of police brutality, excessive force, or civil rights violations, you have the right to hold those responsible accountable. Our network of experienced civil rights attorneys specializes in Section 1983 claims and has successfully recovered millions for victims of law enforcement misconduct.

Your Constitutional Rights

The U.S. Constitution protects you from government overreach, including police misconduct:

  • Fourth Amendment — Protects against unreasonable searches, seizures, and excessive force during arrests
  • Fifth Amendment — Protects your right to remain silent and due process of law
  • Eighth Amendment — Prohibits cruel and unusual punishment
  • Fourteenth Amendment — Guarantees equal protection and due process under state law

When police violate these rights, federal law (42 U.S.C. § 1983) allows you to sue for damages. You can pursue claims against individual officers, supervisors who failed to properly train or supervise, and even the city or county itself in some circumstances.

Types of Police Misconduct Cases We Handle

Our attorneys handle all forms of civil rights violations by law enforcement.

Excessive Force

Beatings, chokeholds, tasings, K-9 attacks, and other force that exceeds what was reasonably necessary given the circumstances.

Police Shootings

Wrongful shootings by police, including cases where unarmed individuals are killed or seriously injured.

Wrongful Arrest

Arrests made without probable cause, based on false information, or in retaliation for exercising constitutional rights.

False Imprisonment

Being detained or held without legal justification, including extended detention without charges.

Illegal Search & Seizure

Searches conducted without a warrant or probable cause, or seizure of property without legal authority.

Malicious Prosecution

Criminal charges brought without probable cause, often to cover up police misconduct.

Racial Profiling

Stops, searches, or arrests based on race, ethnicity, or national origin rather than legitimate suspicion.

Jail & Prison Abuse

Mistreatment of inmates, denial of medical care, and abuse by corrections officers.

First Amendment Retaliation

Arrests or use of force in retaliation for filming police, protesting, or exercising free speech rights.

Police Brutality in Texas

Texas has seen numerous high-profile cases of police misconduct in recent years. From large cities like Houston, Dallas, and San Antonio to smaller communities across the state, incidents of excessive force and civil rights violations continue to occur.

Statute of Limitations

In Texas, you generally have 2 years from the date of the incident to file a Section 1983 civil rights lawsuit. However, certain factors can affect this deadline:

  • Claims against municipalities may require earlier notice of claim
  • Ongoing criminal proceedings may toll (pause) the limitations period in some circumstances
  • If the victim is a minor, the deadline may be extended
  • Discovery of hidden evidence may affect timing

Don't wait—contact an attorney immediately to ensure your rights are protected.

What Damages Can You Recover?

Victims of police brutality may be entitled to recover:

  • Compensatory damages — Medical bills, lost wages, pain and suffering, emotional distress, loss of enjoyment of life
  • Punitive damages — Additional damages to punish egregious misconduct and deter future violations
  • Attorney's fees — Federal civil rights law allows recovery of attorney's fees in successful cases
  • Wrongful death damages — In fatal cases, families may recover funeral expenses, loss of financial support, and loss of companionship

Overcoming Qualified Immunity

One of the biggest obstacles in police brutality cases is "qualified immunity"—a legal doctrine that can shield officers from liability. However, qualified immunity is not absolute:

  • It doesn't apply when officers violate "clearly established" constitutional rights
  • Courts are increasingly finding that excessive force claims overcome immunity
  • Experienced civil rights attorneys know how to build cases that defeat immunity defenses
  • Even when individual officers have immunity, claims against the city may still proceed

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a Section 1983 civil rights claim?
Section 1983 is a federal law that allows individuals to sue government officials, including police officers, for violating their constitutional rights. This includes excessive force, wrongful arrest, unlawful search and seizure, and other civil rights violations committed under color of law.
Can I sue the police for excessive force?
Yes. If police used more force than was reasonably necessary given the circumstances, you may have a claim for excessive force under the Fourth Amendment. You can sue the individual officers and potentially the police department or municipality. An experienced civil rights attorney can evaluate whether the force used was legally excessive.
What if I was arrested—can I still sue?
Being arrested does not prevent you from filing a civil rights lawsuit for excessive force or other misconduct. Even if you were charged with resisting arrest or assault on an officer, you may still have valid claims. Many excessive force victims are charged to justify the officer's conduct. A skilled attorney can pursue your civil case regardless of any criminal charges.
How long do I have to file a police brutality lawsuit?
In Texas, you generally have 2 years from the date of the incident to file a civil rights lawsuit under Section 1983. However, if your claim is against a city or county, you may need to file a notice of claim sooner. Contact an attorney immediately to protect your rights.
What evidence is important in a police brutality case?
Critical evidence includes: body camera and dashcam footage, bystander cell phone videos, witness statements, medical records documenting injuries, police reports, 911 call recordings, and any prior complaints against the officers involved. An attorney can help preserve this evidence before it's lost or destroyed.
What damages can I recover?
Victims may recover compensatory damages (medical bills, lost wages, pain and suffering, emotional distress), punitive damages to punish especially egregious conduct, and attorney's fees under federal civil rights law. In wrongful death cases, families may also recover funeral expenses and loss of companionship.
Will the officer face criminal charges?
Criminal prosecution of police officers is separate from civil lawsuits and is handled by prosecutors, not your attorney. While criminal charges are rare, your civil case can proceed regardless of whether criminal charges are filed. You can still win your civil case even if the officer is not criminally charged.
What is qualified immunity?
Qualified immunity is a legal defense that can protect government officials from civil liability if they did not violate "clearly established" law. However, experienced civil rights attorneys know how to overcome qualified immunity defenses, and courts are increasingly holding officers accountable for excessive force.
