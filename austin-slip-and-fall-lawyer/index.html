Understanding Texas Premises Liability Law

In Texas, property owners and occupiers have a legal duty to maintain safe premises for visitors. When they fail to do so—whether through negligence, carelessness, or cutting corners on maintenance—and someone gets hurt, the property owner can be held liable.

But premises liability cases aren't automatic wins. Texas law requires you to prove specific elements, and property owners and their insurance companies will fight hard to avoid paying. That's why you need an experienced attorney.

What You Must Prove

To win a slip and fall case in Texas, you generally need to prove:

  • A dangerous condition existed — Wet floor, broken stairs, uneven pavement, poor lighting, etc.
  • The property owner knew or should have known — Either they created the hazard, knew about it, or it existed long enough that they should have discovered it
  • They failed to fix it or warn you — Property owners must either repair hazards or provide adequate warnings
  • This failure caused your injury — The dangerous condition was the direct cause of your fall and injuries

Visitor Status Matters

Texas law provides different levels of protection depending on why you were on the property:

  • Invitees (highest protection) — Customers in stores, guests in hotels, visitors to businesses. Property owners owe you the highest duty of care.
  • Licensees (medium protection) — Social guests in homes. Owners must warn of known dangers but don't have to inspect for hidden hazards.
  • Trespassers (lowest protection) — Generally, property owners owe little duty to trespassers, with some exceptions for children.

Common Slip and Fall Hazards

Property owners are responsible for identifying and addressing these dangers.

Wet Floors

Spills, mopping, leaks, rain tracked in from outside—all require immediate cleanup or warning signs.

Uneven Surfaces

Broken sidewalks, uneven flooring transitions, potholes in parking lots, and cracked pavement.

Stairs & Railings

Broken stairs, missing handrails, uneven steps, and poor lighting in stairwells.

Poor Lighting

Dark parking lots, unlit stairwells, and dim corridors that hide hazards.

Cluttered Aisles

Merchandise, boxes, cords, and debris left in walkways create trip hazards.

Weather Conditions

Ice, snow, and standing water that property owners fail to address promptly.

What to Do After a Slip and Fall

The steps you take immediately after a fall can make or break your case:

  • Report the incident — Tell a manager or property owner immediately. Ask for a copy of the incident report.
  • Take photos — Document the hazard that caused your fall, the surrounding area, and your injuries. Do this before anything gets cleaned up or fixed.
  • Get witness information — Names and phone numbers of anyone who saw the fall.
  • Seek medical attention — Even if you think you're okay. Some injuries don't show symptoms immediately.
  • Keep your shoes and clothes — Don't wash or throw away what you were wearing. It's evidence.
  • Don't give recorded statements — Property owners' insurance companies will call. Don't speak to them without an attorney.
  • Contact an attorney quickly — Evidence disappears. Surveillance footage gets erased. Witnesses forget. Act fast.

Common Injuries from Slip and Falls

Falls might seem minor, but they can cause serious, long-lasting injuries:

  • Broken bones — Hip fractures, wrist fractures, and broken ankles are common, especially in older adults
  • Traumatic brain injury — Hitting your head on the floor can cause concussions and more serious brain injuries
  • Spinal cord injuries — Falls can damage vertebrae and discs, leading to chronic pain or paralysis
  • Soft tissue injuries — Sprains, strains, and torn ligaments that may require surgery
  • Knee injuries — Torn ACLs, MCLs, and meniscus damage

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I prove a slip and fall case in Texas?
You must prove: (1) the property owner knew or should have known about the dangerous condition, (2) they failed to fix it or warn you, and (3) this failure caused your injury. Evidence like photos, witness statements, incident reports, and maintenance records are crucial.
What is premises liability?
Premises liability is the legal concept that property owners have a duty to maintain safe conditions for visitors. When they fail to do so and someone is injured, the property owner can be held legally responsible for damages.
What if I was partially at fault for my fall?
Texas follows modified comparative fault rules. You can still recover damages if you were 50% or less at fault, though your compensation will be reduced by your percentage of fault. If you're 51% or more at fault, you cannot recover anything.
How long do I have to file a slip and fall lawsuit?
Texas has a 2-year statute of limitations for premises liability cases, starting from the date of the accident. However, evidence can be lost quickly—surveillance footage is often erased within weeks—so contact an attorney as soon as possible.
The store says it's not their fault. Can I still sue?
Property owners and their insurance companies almost always deny liability initially. That's why you need an attorney who can investigate, gather evidence, and prove their negligence. Many cases that seem weak at first result in significant settlements once the evidence is properly developed.
