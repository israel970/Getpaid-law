Dog Bite Liability Scenarios

Negligent Confinement

Owners who fail to properly fence, leash, or confine dogs that escape and attack can be held liable.

Known Dangerous Dogs

Owners who know their dogs have bitten before or shown aggression have heightened duties to prevent attacks.

Landlord Liability

Landlords who allow tenants to keep known dangerous dogs may share liability for attacks on the property.

Child Victim Attacks

Owners must take extra precautions when children are present, as dogs may react unpredictably to children's behavior.

Postal/Delivery Worker Attacks

Delivery workers are frequent dog bite victims, and owners must secure dogs when expecting visitors.

Trespasser Attacks

Even trespassers may have claims in some circumstances, particularly if owners used dogs as intentional deterrents.

Texas Dog Bite Laws

Understanding Texas law is crucial for maximizing your recovery. Here are key legal principles affecting dog bite cases:

  • One Bite Rule: Texas traditionally required proof the dog had previously bitten or shown viciousness. However, owners can be liable under negligence even for first attacks if they failed to use reasonable care.
  • Negligence Per Se: Violations of leash laws or other animal control ordinances can establish negligence per se, making liability easier to prove.
  • Dangerous Dog Act: Texas has a Dangerous Dog Act requiring registration and confinement of dogs declared dangerous by authorities. Violations strengthen injury claims.
  • Homeowner's Insurance: Most dog bite claims are paid by homeowner's or renter's insurance, which typically covers liability for pet attacks up to policy limits.

Frequently Asked Questions About Austin Dog Bites

What should I do after a dog attack?

Seek immediate medical attention—dog bites easily become infected. Report the attack to local animal control. Get the owner's name, address, and insurance information. Photograph your injuries. Document the dog's breed and any prior aggressive behavior you know about.

How do I prove liability in a dog bite case?

We investigate whether the owner knew the dog was dangerous, whether they violated leash laws or confinement requirements, and whether they took reasonable precautions. Witness statements, animal control records, and prior incident reports help establish liability.

What compensation can I receive for a dog bite?

Compensation includes medical expenses (often extensive due to infection risk and scarring), plastic surgery costs, lost wages, pain and suffering, psychological trauma, and sometimes punitive damages if the owner knew the dog was dangerous.

Does the dog owner's insurance cover attacks?

Usually yes. Homeowner's and renter's insurance typically cover dog bite liability up to policy limits. Some policies exclude certain breeds, but the owner may still be personally liable.

Where do dog attacks commonly occur in Austin?

Dog attacks in Austin occur throughout residential neighborhoods, at parks, on sidewalks, and at the homes of dog owners. Children are often attacked while playing in yards or walking to school.

Where should I go for treatment after a dog bite in Austin?

Seek immediate medical attention—emergency rooms can handle serious bites. Dell Seton Medical Center at The University of Texas treats dog attack injuries. Dog bites require thorough cleaning, antibiotics, and tetanus updates. Watch for signs of infection in following days.

What if the dog had never bitten anyone before?

Texas's 'one bite rule' isn't absolute. If the owner was negligent—failing to leash the dog, ignoring aggressive behavior, or violating local ordinances—liability can exist even for first-time attacks.

Can I sue if the dog owner is a friend or family member?

Yes. Their homeowner's insurance typically pays claims, not them personally. Many dog bite claims involve known dogs because people interact closely with friends' and relatives' pets.
