Understanding Illegal Search & Seizure Claims

The Fourth Amendment protects you from unreasonable searches and seizures. When police search your home, car, or person without a warrant or valid exception, they violate your constitutional rights.

Beyond criminal defense, you may have a civil rights claim for damages when police conduct illegal searches. Our attorneys hold officers accountable for overstepping their authority.

Types of Illegal Search & Seizure Cases

Warrantless Home Searches

Police entering and searching your home without a valid warrant.

Vehicle Searches

Illegal searches of your car during traffic stops without probable cause.

Stop and Frisk

Unlawful pat-downs without reasonable suspicion of weapons.

Property Seizure

Police taking your property without proper legal authority.

Cell Phone Searches

Accessing your phone data without a warrant.

Strip Searches

Humiliating and often unnecessary invasive searches.

Filing a Illegal Search & Seizure Lawsuit

Civil rights claims against police are brought under 42 U.S.C. ยง 1983, which allows victims to sue government officials who violate constitutional rights. Key elements include:

  • Constitutional Violation: Proving the officer violated a specific constitutional right
  • Acting Under Color of Law: The officer was acting in their official capacity
  • Qualified Immunity: Overcoming the officers defense that their conduct was not clearly unconstitutional
  • Municipal Liability: Potentially holding the department liable for patterns of misconduct

Frequently Asked Questions

When do police need a warrant to search?

Generally, police need a warrant to search your home. Exceptions include consent, plain view, emergency situations, and searches incident to arrest.

Can police search my car without a warrant?

Police can search your car if they have probable cause to believe it contains evidence of a crime. They cannot search based on a traffic violation alone.

What if evidence was found during an illegal search?

Illegally obtained evidence is typically excluded from criminal trials. You may also have a civil claim for the unconstitutional search.

Can I refuse a search?

You have the right to refuse consent to a search. Clearly state: I do not consent to this search. Officers may search anyway if they have other justification.
