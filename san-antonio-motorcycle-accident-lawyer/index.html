The Unique Challenges of Motorcycle Accident Cases

Motorcycle accident cases are different from car accident cases in several important ways. The injuries are usually more severe, the bias against riders is real, and insurance companies use specific tactics to minimize payouts to motorcyclists.

The Anti-Motorcycle Bias Is Real

Let's be honest: many people—including insurance adjusters, jurors, and even some judges—have a built-in bias against motorcyclists. They assume riders are reckless thrill-seekers who "had it coming." This bias can significantly reduce the value of your claim if it's not addressed head-on.

Insurance companies exploit this bias. They'll argue you were speeding (even if you weren't), weaving through traffic (even if you were riding safely), or "hard to see" (as if that's your fault). They'll try to shift blame onto you to reduce what they pay.

An attorney who understands motorcycles can effectively counter this bias. They know how to explain lane positioning, defensive riding techniques, and the physics of motorcycle crashes in ways that resonate with adjusters and jurors.

More Severe Injuries Mean More at Stake

Motorcyclists don't have the protection of a steel cage, airbags, or seatbelts. When a car hits a motorcycle, the rider takes the full force of the impact. Common motorcycle accident injuries include:

  • Road rash — From minor abrasions to deep wounds requiring skin grafts
  • Broken bones — Especially legs, arms, wrists, and collarbones
  • Traumatic brain injuries — Even with a helmet, TBI is common in motorcycle crashes
  • Spinal cord injuries — Including partial or complete paralysis
  • Internal injuries — Organ damage from blunt force trauma
  • Amputations — Limbs crushed or severed in severe collisions

These serious injuries result in extensive medical bills, long rehabilitation periods, and potentially permanent disability. The stakes are simply higher in motorcycle cases.

San Antonio's Riding Environment

San Antonio offers excellent riding year-round thanks to its mild climate, but the roads present real challenges:

  • I-10 and I-35: High-speed highways with heavy traffic, distracted drivers, and aggressive commercial trucks
  • Loop 410 and Loop 1604: Constant lane changes, merge conflicts, and drivers unfamiliar with the area
  • Downtown and River Walk area: Heavy pedestrian traffic, tourists in rental cars, and rideshare vehicles making unpredictable stops
  • Military base traffic: Shift changes at Fort Sam Houston, Lackland, and Randolph create traffic surges with distracted, tired drivers
  • Hill Country rides: Popular routes to Boerne, Bandera, and the wine country bring inexperienced riders and scenic-distracted drivers
  • Tourist traffic: Out-of-town visitors unfamiliar with local roads and traffic patterns

Common Types of San Antonio Motorcycle Accidents

Our network handles all types of motorcycle crashes throughout the San Antonio area.

Left-Turn Accidents

The most common and most dangerous. A driver turns left in front of an oncoming motorcycle, claiming they "didn't see" the rider. This is negligence, plain and simple.

Lane Change Collisions

A driver changes lanes without checking their blind spot and strikes a motorcycle. Especially common on crowded highways like Loop 410 and I-10.

Rear-End Crashes

A distracted driver fails to notice a stopped or slowing motorcycle and rear-ends it. Even a low-speed rear-end can be catastrophic for a rider.

Right Hook Accidents

A driver turns right directly in front of a motorcycle traveling in the same direction, cutting off the rider and causing a collision.

Door Strikes

A parked driver opens their door into traffic without looking, striking a passing motorcycle. Common in downtown and tourist areas.

Road Hazard Crashes

Potholes, debris, gravel, and oil slicks that cars can drive over can cause motorcycles to crash. Responsible parties may include government entities or private property owners.

Military Traffic Accidents

San Antonio's large military population means many service members ride motorcycles. Accidents involving military personnel or near military bases may have unique considerations.

Group Ride Accidents

When a driver strikes a group of riders, multiple injuries and complex liability issues arise. These cases require experienced coordination.

Hit and Run

Drivers who hit motorcyclists and flee the scene are unfortunately common. Your UM/UIM coverage can provide compensation while police investigate.

Texas Motorcycle Laws You Should Know

Helmet Laws

Texas requires helmets for riders under 21. Riders 21 and over can ride without a helmet if they:

  • Have completed a motorcycle safety course, OR
  • Are covered by a health insurance plan that covers motorcycle accident injuries

If you were riding legally without a helmet and suffered a head injury, the insurance company may still try to use it against you. An experienced attorney knows how to handle this argument.

Lane Splitting Is Illegal in Texas

Unlike California, Texas does not allow lane splitting (riding between lanes of traffic). If you were lane splitting when an accident occurred, it could affect your claim—though the other driver may still share significant fault.

Comparative Fault

Texas follows modified comparative fault. You can recover damages if you're 50% or less at fault, but your recovery is reduced by your percentage of fault. Insurance companies will try to inflate your fault percentage—especially with motorcyclists.

Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist Coverage

Given Texas's high rate of uninsured drivers, UM/UIM coverage is critical for motorcyclists. If the at-fault driver has no insurance or insufficient coverage, your own UM/UIM policy can fill the gap.

What Compensation Can You Recover?

Texas law allows motorcycle accident victims to recover full compensation for their losses:

Economic Damages

  • Medical expenses — Emergency care, surgery, hospitalization, rehabilitation, and ongoing treatment
  • Lost wages — Income lost while recovering from your injuries
  • Loss of earning capacity — If your injuries prevent you from returning to your previous job
  • Motorcycle damage — Repair or replacement of your bike, plus gear and accessories
  • Future medical costs — Projected costs for ongoing care, therapy, and medical equipment

Non-Economic Damages

  • Pain and suffering — Compensation for physical pain from your injuries
  • Mental anguish — Emotional distress, anxiety, PTSD, and depression
  • Loss of enjoyment — Inability to ride or participate in activities you enjoyed
  • Disfigurement — Scarring and permanent physical changes
  • Physical impairment — Reduced mobility or physical capabilities

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a motorcycle-specific attorney for my San Antonio crash?
While any personal injury attorney can technically handle a motorcycle case, attorneys who understand motorcycles can better counter the bias against riders. They understand lane positioning, defensive riding, and can effectively argue against claims that you were "reckless" simply for being on a motorcycle.
Will my case be hurt because I wasn't wearing a helmet?
Texas allows riders 21+ to go without helmets if they meet certain requirements. If you were legal and weren't wearing a helmet, it generally can't be used against you for injuries unrelated to your head. However, insurance companies will try to use it anyway—an experienced attorney is crucial to counter this tactic.
The driver says they didn't see me. Does that help their case?
"I didn't see the motorcycle" is not a legal defense—it's an admission of negligence. Drivers have a duty to look for all vehicles, including motorcycles. If they failed to see you and caused an accident, they're liable. A good attorney knows how to frame this to your advantage.
How long do I have to file a motorcycle accident lawsuit in Texas?
You have 2 years from the date of the accident to file a lawsuit. However, evidence disappears quickly—skid marks fade, witnesses forget, and surveillance footage gets erased. Contact an attorney as soon as possible to preserve critical evidence.
What if the at-fault driver doesn't have insurance?
Texas has one of the highest uninsured driver rates in the country. Your own uninsured motorist (UM) coverage can provide compensation if the at-fault driver has no insurance. If they have some coverage but not enough, your underinsured motorist (UIM) coverage can fill the gap. An attorney can identify all available sources of compensation.
Should I accept the insurance company's settlement offer?
Almost never accept an early settlement offer. Insurance companies make quick, low offers hoping you'll settle before understanding the full extent of your injuries. Motorcycle injuries often require extensive ongoing treatment. Once you accept a settlement, you can't ask for more—even if your injuries turn out to be worse than expected.
I'm active duty military and was injured in a motorcycle accident. Are there special considerations?
Military personnel have unique considerations including TRICARE coverage, potential impact on military career, and specific base regulations. An attorney familiar with San Antonio's military community can navigate these issues effectively.
