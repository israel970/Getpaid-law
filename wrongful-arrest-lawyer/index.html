Understanding Wrongful Arrest Claims

The Fourth Amendment protects you from arrest without probable cause. When police arrest you without reasonable basis to believe you committed a crime, they violate your constitutional rights.

Wrongful arrests cause more than just inconvenience. Victims may lose their jobs, suffer reputational harm, and experience lasting psychological effects from unjust incarceration.

Types of Wrongful Arrest Cases

Arrests Without Probable Cause

Police making arrests without sufficient evidence of criminal activity.

Mistaken Identity Arrests

Being arrested for someone elses crime based on false identification.

Retaliatory Arrests

Arrests made to punish lawful activities like filming police or exercising free speech.

Discriminatory Arrests

Arrests based on race, religion, or other protected characteristics.

Unlawful Traffic Stops

Arrests stemming from traffic stops made without reasonable suspicion.

Warrant Errors

Arrests based on invalid, expired, or mistaken warrants.

Filing a Wrongful Arrest Lawsuit

Civil rights claims against police are brought under 42 U.S.C. ยง 1983, which allows victims to sue government officials who violate constitutional rights. Key elements include:

  • Constitutional Violation: Proving the officer violated a specific constitutional right
  • Acting Under Color of Law: The officer was acting in their official capacity
  • Qualified Immunity: Overcoming the officers defense that their conduct was not clearly unconstitutional
  • Municipal Liability: Potentially holding the department liable for patterns of misconduct

Frequently Asked Questions

What is probable cause?

Probable cause means the officer has reasonable grounds to believe you committed a crime based on facts and circumstances, not just hunches or suspicion.

What damages can I recover for wrongful arrest?

You may recover compensation for time spent in custody, lost wages, emotional distress, damage to reputation, and legal fees to defend against charges.

What if charges were dropped?

Dropped charges actually strengthen your wrongful arrest case by demonstrating the arrest lacked merit.

Can I sue if I was guilty of a minor offense?

If you were arrested for something you did not do, you can sue even if you were separately guilty of a minor offense.
