Understanding Malicious Prosecution Claims

Malicious prosecution occurs when criminal charges are brought against you without probable cause and for improper purposes. Victims endure months or years of legal proceedings for crimes they did not commit.

When prosecutors or police knowingly pursue baseless charges, or hide evidence of innocence, they can be held civilly liable. These cases require proving the prosecution was initiated without probable cause and with malicious intent.

Types of Malicious Prosecution Cases

Fabricated Evidence

Charges based on evidence police or prosecutors knew was false.

Hidden Exculpatory Evidence

Prosecutors concealing evidence that would prove your innocence.

Witness Coercion

Police or prosecutors pressuring witnesses to give false testimony.

Retaliatory Prosecution

Charges filed to punish you for exercising constitutional rights.

Prosecutorial Misconduct

Prosecutors violating ethical rules and legal standards.

Wrongful Convictions

Civil claims after overturning wrongful convictions.

Filing a Malicious Prosecution Lawsuit

Civil rights claims against police are brought under 42 U.S.C. ยง 1983, which allows victims to sue government officials who violate constitutional rights. Key elements include:

  • Constitutional Violation: Proving the officer violated a specific constitutional right
  • Acting Under Color of Law: The officer was acting in their official capacity
  • Qualified Immunity: Overcoming the officers defense that their conduct was not clearly unconstitutional
  • Municipal Liability: Potentially holding the department liable for patterns of misconduct

Frequently Asked Questions

What must I prove for malicious prosecution?

You must prove: (1) criminal proceedings were initiated against you, (2) they ended in your favor, (3) there was no probable cause, and (4) the prosecution was motivated by malice.

Can I sue a prosecutor?

Prosecutors have immunity for actions within their prosecutorial role, but may be liable for investigative actions or actions outside the scope of prosecution.

What if charges were dismissed?

Dismissal of charges supports your case by showing the prosecution ended in your favor, a required element.

What damages can I recover?

You can recover for legal fees, lost wages, damage to reputation, emotional distress, and in egregious cases, punitive damages.
